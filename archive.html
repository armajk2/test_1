Research Findings

[1]

It's better to tap into markets that already exist. Platforms like 숨고 [Korean service marketplace that connects users with local professionals.] and 크몽몽 [a freelance marketplace for digital and creative services.] reveal a strong demand, especially for freelance skills like design and tutoring. Marketing takes up 80% of the effort. Helping solo entrepreneurs is fast to launch. However, platform models are tough—they require both active users [ones who use the service in real life.] and providers. [the ones offering the service. in other words, us ]

[2]

You have two paths: sell your skill and scale, or build a user-driven platform. With limited time and no capital, skill-based business is easier short-term. But platform-based ideas have longer-term potential. Your language skills (KR/EN/JP) open overseas options too.

[3]

Focus on building something for the future. Ask yourself: how much value can you give, to how many people? Unique skills [its all about the money] are key to differentiation. Build from your core expertise, not just trendy ideas. Don't aim for a one-season hit—go for sustainable growth.

[4]

You're not creating a dating app. You're building something for connection and cultural joy. People crave friends, hobbies, belonging, and new experiences. The feeling of 설렘 [A heart-fluttering excitement, especially when meeting someone new.]—thrill and discovery—is your secret weapon. Position your product around shared joy and cultural connection.

[5]

Think: do you sell your skill, or create a space where others sell theirs? You can consult, design, or tutor, but scaling is tricky. Platforms like facebook [an advertising-based business model.] or Air bnb [a platform-based commission model] grew by connecting people. Consider doing both: sell now, build later. Stay aware of gender/culture balance, and clarify how you're different from existing apps.

Key Use Cases

User Registration

New users can create an account and set up their profile.

Steps:

  1. User clicks "Sign Up"
  2. User enters email and password
  3. User verifies email
  4. User completes profile setup

Creating Posts

Users can create and publish new posts to the community.

Steps:

  1. User clicks "Write Post"
  2. User selects category
  3. User writes content
  4. User adds media (optional)
  5. User publishes post

Searching Content

Users can search for specific content within the platform.

Steps:

  1. User enters search term
  2. System displays relevant results
  3. User can filter results
  4. User selects desired content

Timeline

Project Kickoff

Today marked the beginning of our exciting journey. The team gathered for the first time, sharing ideas and setting ambitious goals. We discussed the potential impact of our platform on the exchange student community, focusing on creating meaningful connections and cultural experiences. The energy in the room was palpable as we outlined our vision for the next few months.

Idea Validation

The business process starts with validating whether the idea is practical through direct questions and Q&A over 7 days. This is followed by market research—analyzing existing similar services (apps/websites), their user base, pain points, and revenue to see if there's room for improvement and a reason for users to switch. Afterward, we predict usage by testing interest via surveys or subscription models. Three idea directions include: a university sponsorship platform, a graduate school info-sharing site, and an app targeting loneliness. The core strategy is to build upon markets with existing demand, offering a better or more focused experience. It's critical to assess legal risks and truly understand user frustration. Business value starts with insight—don’t just ask “can we do it?” but “should we do it?” Dive in or walk away—great ideas come from sharp observations, even if you're broke and living off your parents (aka a disgraceful freeloader).

Target & Tone

The platform targets exchange students who want to connect with Koreans, aims to generate revenue through ads and future expansions, and is designed with a friendly, bright mood (yellow, green), focusing on experiences of excitement, companionship, and meaningful connections in Korea.

Initial Research Phase

After conducting extensive market research and user interviews, we've identified key pain points in the exchange student experience. The data shows a strong need for a platform that bridges cultural gaps and facilitates meaningful connections. Our findings suggest that current solutions lack the personal touch and community aspect that exchange students crave.

Design Sprint

We completed an intensive design sprint, focusing on user experience and interface design. The team created multiple wireframes and prototypes, testing different approaches to navigation and content organization. User feedback was overwhelmingly positive, particularly regarding the intuitive layout and engaging visual elements we incorporated.

Development Milestone

The core functionality of our platform is now operational. We've implemented the user authentication system, content management features, and real-time messaging capabilities. The development team has made significant progress in optimizing performance and ensuring cross-platform compatibility. Initial testing shows promising results in terms of speed and reliability.

Task Updates and Fixes

To-do items include displaying an "Email confirmation required" message and enabling email re-verification. Current tasks involve fixing like and emoji issues in comments, updating the bio popup, and establishing a font hierarchy. Donggyun has resolved issues with the bio not displaying and completed the signup process.

Beta Testing Launch

We've officially launched our beta testing phase with a select group of exchange students. The feedback has been incredibly valuable, helping us identify areas for improvement and unexpected use cases. Users particularly appreciate the community features and the ease of finding local events and activities. We're now working on implementing the suggested improvements.

Review / Reflection

What Went Well

The features were implemented well. Although it took about a month longer than expected, I believe that all the features we aimed to implement turned out great in the end. The design color turned out better than expected, and most of all, I am really satisfied with the brand name we came up with. Additionally, through role division, we each did our tasks very well, and I believe we have all grown from the experience.

Challenges Faced

The biggest challenge was diving in with the belief that we could easily create something using AI without a solid foundation. No matter how advanced AI gets, we realized that we have to understand and modify it ourselves to truly implement the features we want. That was really difficult. Also, when I designed it in Figma, everything seemed easy, but when I tried to implement it with code, the layout wasn’t coming out as I wanted, and the features weren’t working, which was very frustrating. Originally, we planned to finish by March, but it ended up getting pushed to April. It was really disappointing.

Lessons Learned

AI still cannot replace humans—at least not yet. Trying to build a fully responsive website was an overly ambitious challenge. There’s so much to study in UI/UX. But once you understand the theory, growth feels much faster. Also, when working on tasks, I realized that sharing files before one person is fully done can lead to errors—either someone else modifies it incorrectly, or I do. I’ve learned that it’s best to let one person finish before the next begins. Still, it was a valuable experience overall.

Future Improvements

Since the design isn’t fully complete, we still need to keep refining the visual details. Currently, it only works well on mobile, so we’ll need to continue improving it to look good on web as well. We also realized the importance of communication. In the future, we plan to collect real user feedback and keep iterating based on that input. Additionally, the 3D interactions are still too heavy, so they’ll require a lot more optimization going forward.
